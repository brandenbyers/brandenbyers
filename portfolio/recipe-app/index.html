The design of a recipe management app.

Working on the design of a recipe management app for iOS, I emphasized creating an app with mise en place (a French culinary term meaning "everything in its place"), where a user could easily put in and pull out recipes without distraction or a cluttered interface; the same as a chef on the line at a packed restaurant.

The goal was to create a modern and attractive layout that simplified the process of managing recipes on iOS and iPadOS (and eventually macOS). The app was designed with a focus on efficient navigation, organization, and preparation to ensure user success throughout the app and in the kitchen.

Instead of using Sketch to mock up this design, I explored using SwiftUI (Apple's new declarative approach to front-end coding) as a prototyping and design tool. Because of this explority design via code, there were no wireframes. Overall, it worked surprisingly well and I plan to use a similar approach in future iOS projects.

From the onset, this app was designed with both light and dark modes in mind.

Given that this app will be released with support for iOS 13 and above, it was imperative to fulfill this basic user expectation. Designing for dark mode also differentiates this app from other recipe management apps that have not adopted dark mode support.

The app launches to the recipes list view where the user can view, sort, and search for stored recipes.

The app supports adding a recipe via the "Add Recipe" button in the top navigation bar where a user is prompted to enter a recipe manually or paste a url for direct import. The app also supports import bookmarklets and native iOS share features as alternative means of adding recipes when the app is not active.

In the recipe detail view, if the recipe has an image, it will be fit to display full width at the top of the screen. The image is intentionally small so as not to distract from quick access to recipe details.

The recipe description is truncated to a maximum of two lines. If a user wishes to re-read the description (they most likely already read it before originally importing the recipe), then tapping on the description will expand it to full line length.

This may be a food app, but you won't find any hamburger menus (nor tab bars).

The only forms of navigation are via the navigation bar, in-view buttons, and native iOS swiping gestures. This further empowers the user to flow through the app unencumbered by countless taps and navigation.
